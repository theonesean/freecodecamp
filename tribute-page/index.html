Dr. Thomas Starzl, M.D., Ph.D.

Father of modern transplantation

Dr. Starzl in his office at the University of Pittsburgh.

Here's a timeline of Dr. Starzl's life:

  • 11 March 1926: Born in Le Mars, Iowa, to newspaper editor and sci-fi author Roman Frederick Starzl, and teacher and nurse Anna Laura Fitzgerald.
  • 1944-45: Spends a short stint in the Navy before entering university.
  • 1947: Starzl's mother, Anna, dies of breast cancer. Her respect for the doctors that treated her during her terminal illness would inspire Starzl, who originally wanted to be a priest, to study medicine.
  • 1947: Graduates with a B.A. in Biology from Westminster College in Missouri.
  • 1952: Starzl completes his medical school training, having obtained a M.S. in Anatomy (1950), a Ph.D. in Neurophysiology, and an M.D. with distinction from Northwestern University Medical School in Chicago, IL. It was during this time that he wrote a highly-cited paper which describes a novel technique to record the brain's response to sensory stimuli.
  • 1952-59: Trains as a surgeon at Johns Hopkins, the University of Miami, and Northwestern University.
  • 1959-61: Serves on the surgical faculty of Northwestern University, supported in his first five years by a prestigious fellowship for promising young doctors, the Markle Scholarship.
  • 1962: Begins his 18-year tenure at the University of Colorado, during which time he would serve as the Director of Surgery at the Denver VA hospital and Chairman of the University Surgery Department.
  • 1967: After laying the theoretical foundation for organ transplantation during his post-doctoral studies, Starzl performs the first successful liver transplant on a 19-month-old girl suffering from liver cancer.
  • 1981: Moves from the University of Colorado to the University of Pittsburgh, freeing him from administrative responsibility and leaving him to develop a multiple organ transplantation program.
  • 1983: Establishes the clinical utility of cyclosporine, the first FDA-approved immunosuppressant for use in organ transplants. The drug prevents organ rejection by limiting the immune system of the host, and continues to be widely used.
  • 1984: Performs the first simultaneous heart and liver transplant on Stormie Jones, a six-year-old whose story made her a media sensation.
  • 1991: Retires from surgical practice, but remains active as a research professor.
  • 1992: Publishes his memoir, The Puzzle People, about his patients and life as a transplant surgeon.
  • 1999: The Institute for Scientific Information documents Starzl as the most cited researcher in the world.
  • 4 March 2017: Dies peacefully in his home in Pittsburgh, with his wife of 36 years, Joy, and his family.
